Watermill Scroll

This demo replaces normal browser scrolling with the watermill on the right. Drag the hose, let the water hit the wheel, and the page will move.

How it works

The hose hangs in the fixed column and can be dragged around inside it. While you hold the nozzle, water particles stream out, collide with the mill, and turn that motion into page scroll.

Interaction

Pull the nozzle lower for a stronger stream. Water hitting the right side of the mill turns it one way and scrolls down. Water hitting the left side turns it back and scrolls up.

If you try to use the mouse wheel or keyboard instead, the page shows a reminder to use the hose.

Ancient Origins: The Greek and Roman Wheels

The earliest recorded watermills dates back to the 3rd century BCE, appearing in the Hellenistic period. These initial designs were horizontal wheels, often called "Norse mills," which featured a simple paddle wheel connected directly to a millstone.

The Romans later perfected the vertical wheel using gears to transfer power. This leap in engineering, famously described by Vitruvius, allowed for much higher torque and efficiency, paving the way for large-scale flour production across the Roman Empire.

The Domesday Book and Medieval Proliferation

Following the fall of Rome, watermill technology didn't vanish; it flourished. By the time of the Domesday Book in 1086, England alone recorded over 6,000 active watermills. They became the heart of the medieval manorial economy.

During this era, the "overshot" wheel became more common. By using gravity to pull water down onto the buckets of a wheel from above, millers could generate significantly more power than the traditional "undershot" design used in fast-flowing rivers.

Diversification of Use: Beyond Grain

By the 12th and 13th centuries, watermills were no longer just for grinding corn. Engineers adapted the camshaft to convert rotary motion into reciprocal motion. This led to the creation of fulling mills for textiles, forge mills for ironworking, and sawmills for timber.

This period marked the first real "industrial revolution," where human and animal labor were systematically replaced by hydraulic power, fundamentally changing the landscape of European manufacturing and labor.

The Industrial Revolution and the Iron Wheel

In the 18th century, John Smeaton applied scientific principles to watermill design, proving through experimentation that overshot wheels were far more efficient than undershot ones. This led to the construction of massive iron wheels that powered the early textile giants of the North of England.

The watermill provided the bridge to the modern age. Even as steam engines began to take over, massive water-powered sites like Quarry Bank Mill demonstrated the sheer scale of energy that could be harvested from a controlled river.

The Transition to Hydroelectric Power

The final chapter in the history of the watermill is its evolution into the water turbine. In the mid-19th century, engineers like Benoît Fourneyron developed enclosed turbines that could spin at high speeds with incredible efficiency.

This technology directly evolved into the hydroelectric dams we see today. While the quaint wooden wheel in the river has mostly become a relic of heritage, the fundamental principle of capturing the kinetic energy of falling water remains a cornerstone of global renewable energy.

Notes

The widget uses a single canvas for rendering and Matter.js for hose and particle simulation. The page disables native scrolling while the demo is active.

Resize the window to see the column switch to the smaller floating layout.

Implementation details

The script tracks its event listeners, supports full teardown through `destroy()`, and rebuilds the simulation when the layout changes.
